Stars begin as dense regions within interstellar gas and dust clouds, known as stellar nurseries.These clouds undergo gravitational collapse, triggered by factors like shockwaves from nearby supernovae or the gravitational interaction with other clouds.

When the core of a protostar reaches a critical temperature and pressure, the process of nuclear fusion begins, marking the start of the main sequence phase in a star's life. This phase, which is the longest and most stable, involves the fusion of hydrogen into helium in the core, enabling the star to generate energy.

Throughout the main sequence phase, stars experience gradual transformations that depend on their initial mass. Low-mass stars, including our Sun, will eventually expand into red giants, shed their outer layers, and transform into white dwarfs. On the other hand, higher-mass stars can evolve into red supergiants, undergo explosive supernova events, and leave behind remnants such as neutron stars or black holes.

In the final stages of a star's lifecycle, what happens next is determined by its mass. Low-mass stars will shed their outer layers and become white dwarfs, which are dense and hot cores that gradually cool over billions of years. Conversely, high-mass stars conclude their lives with extraordinary supernova explosions, dispersing heavy elements into space and leaving behind remnants such as neutron stars or black holes.
